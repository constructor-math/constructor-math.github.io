The Mathematics of Cryptography

From simple ciphers to secure internet communication, it's all number theory.

Cryptography is the art of keeping secrets, and at its core it is built on some of the deepest ideas in mathematics. From the humble Caesar cipher to the public‑key systems that protect your messages, number theory, modular arithmetic, and algebraic structures do the heavy lifting.

Classical Roots

Why does your online banking password feel like gibberish? Because it is backed by math. The Caesar Cipher represents the historical origin of encryption, using simple alphabetic shifts to secure military communication.

Key insight: A one-way function is easy to compute but hard to invert — the foundation of secure encryption.

Wait, So What's Happening?

Every cryptographic algorithm hides a mathematical puzzle in plain sight. For example, RSA relies on the mathematical difficulty of factoring large semi-prime numbers (the product of two large primes, P and Q).

Modular Arithmetic and Congruence

We utilize Euler's totient function $\phi(n)$, the Greatest Common Divisor (GCD), and modular remainders to create keys:

  • Public Key: Used by the sender to encrypt the message.
  • Private Key: Kept secret by the receiver to decrypt the message.
Explaining the transition to quantum computing.

Post-Quantum Solutions

Lattice-Based Cryptography uses the "Geometry of Numbers"—linear combinations of basis vectors in a D-dimensional space—to create security that resists quantum attacks.

Hard Mathematical Problems

  • Shortest Vector Problem (SVP): Finding the vector in a lattice closest to the origin.
  • Closest Vector Problem (CVP): Finding the lattice point closest to a random point in space.

Learning With Errors (LWE)

This method encrypts data by adding "noise" or errors to linear equations, making them computationally "hard" to solve even for quantum computers. Alice and Bob communicate using matrices and error distributions to ensure indistinguishable ciphertexts.

Future Outlook

"Harvest Now, Decrypt Later": Adversaries are collecting encrypted data today to decrypt it once quantum hardware matures. Global mandates are pushing for an immediate shift to Post-Quantum Cryptography (PQC) to secure long-term data privacy.
