When Mathematics Reaches for the Stars

How Interstellar Used Real Physics to Bend Time and Space

Did time really slow down during that movie, or were we just mesmerized by the spectacle?

This was the second episode of "The Math Behind..." series, where we explored the mathematics and physics behind Christopher Nolan's Interstellar. Presented by Pratixit (freshman) and Tushar (second-year), the seminar tackled one of cinema's most scientifically ambitious films—a movie that didn't just use physics as window dressing, but as the very foundation of its storytelling.

What Makes Interstellar Special: Unlike most sci-fi films, Interstellar collaborated with Nobel Prize-winning physicist Kip Thorne, who helped create accurate visualizations of black holes and wormholes. When the first real black hole image was captured years later, it looked remarkably similar to the film's Gargantua.

The Film That Dared to Calculate

Interstellar tells the story of Cooper, a former NASA pilot who ventures through a wormhole near Saturn to find humanity a new home. But what elevates the film beyond typical science fiction is its commitment to scientific accuracy. Every spectacular visual—from the warped spacetime near black holes to the time dilation on water worlds—is based on real physics.

Music: Hans Zimmer's iconic score features ticking at 1.25-second intervals—each tick representing one day passing on Earth while the crew is on Miller's planet.

Time Since Release: Since Interstellar premiered in 2014, approximately 1 hour and 34 minutes have passed on Miller's planet (while over a decade has passed for us).

Scientific Advisor: Kip Thorne didn't just advise—he helped create the equations that made Gargantua look so realistic, giving visual effects artists the tools to render what a real black hole would look like.

Question 1: The Mystery of Miller's Planet

One of the film's most dramatic scenes occurs on Miller's planet, where the crew experiences extreme time dilation. On this water world, 1 hour equals 7 years on Earth. The planet also features massive tidal waves—1.2 kilometers high—caused by the gravitational pull of Gargantua, the supermassive black hole it orbits.

What the Mathematics Tells Us

Using principles from physics, we can calculate how far Miller's planet should be from Gargantua to create those massive tidal waves. The answer? Approximately 4.5 billion kilometers from the black hole's center.

But here's where it gets interesting: when we use Einstein's theories to calculate how much time should slow down at that distance, the math tells us something different from what the movie shows.

The Movie Says: 1 hour on Miller's planet = 7 years on Earth
The Math Says: At 4.5 billion km, time would only slow down by about 5%

The Verdict: To get the dramatic time difference shown in the movie, Miller's planet would need to be so close to Gargantua that it would be torn apart by gravity—or already inside the black hole itself.

This is where Christopher Nolan and Kip Thorne made a conscious choice: prioritize emotional impact over strict accuracy. And honestly? It works. The tension of watching those waves approach while knowing decades are passing on Earth creates one of cinema's most anxiety-inducing sequences.

Question 2: Why Wasn't Cooper Torn Apart?

When Cooper falls into Gargantua, something miraculous happens: instead of being destroyed, he finds himself in a strange five-dimensional space—a tesseract where he can see and interact with different moments in his daughter Murph's life simultaneously.

In reality, the gravitational forces near a black hole should have torn him apart in a process scientists call "spaghettification." So how does the movie explain his survival?

Higher Dimensions and Compressed Space

The film uses ideas from advanced physics about higher dimensions. The concept is that our universe might be like a three-dimensional sheet (called a "brane") floating in a space with more dimensions that we can't normally perceive.

When Cooper falls through Gargantua, he enters one of these higher-dimensional spaces where the rules are different. In this space, distances can be compressed—imagine taking a long piece of paper and crumpling it into a ball. Points that were far apart on the flat paper are now close together in 3D space.

The Distance Compression Effect: In our normal universe, Gargantua and Earth are approximately 10 billion light-years apart. But in the higher-dimensional space Cooper enters, that distance gets compressed down to about 150 million kilometers—roughly the distance from Earth to the Sun. That's a compression of about 67 million to 1!

The Tesseract Explained

The five-dimensional tesseract allows Cooper to see time as a physical dimension he can move through, much like we can walk through space. He sees Murph's bedroom as a three-dimensional object that exists across all moments in time, allowing him to reach into different points simultaneously.

This visualization is based on real mathematical concepts about how beings in higher dimensions would perceive our four-dimensional spacetime (three space dimensions plus time).

Where Science Fiction Takes Over

While Interstellar gets a lot right, even Kip Thorne acknowledges that some liberties were taken for dramatic effect:

The Communication Problem

Even with compressed space, Cooper's messages to Murph appear to arrive instantly. In reality, information— even gravitational waves—can't travel faster than the speed of light. The film never fully explains how Cooper bypasses this fundamental limit of physics.

The Survival Problem

While the movie invokes higher dimensions to explain Cooper's survival, there's no established scientific theory that explains why entering these dimensions would prevent him from being destroyed by the black hole's gravitational forces.

The Bottom Line: Interstellar pushes real physics as far as it can go, then takes a creative leap for the story. That's what makes it science fiction rather than a documentary—and that's okay! The important part is that it introduces audiences to real concepts and inspires curiosity about the universe.

What Makes This Film Special

1. Science Can Be Spectacular: Interstellar proves that grounding a film in real physics doesn't make it less exciting—it makes it more awe-inspiring. The visualization of Gargantua was so accurate that when scientists captured the first real black hole image, it looked remarkably similar.

2. Mathematics Describes the Impossible: Even though we can't travel through black holes, mathematics allows us to explore what would happen if we could. This is the power of theoretical physics— using math to understand realms we can't physically reach.

3. Details Matter: From Zimmer's ticking score to the accurate rendering of gravitational lensing around Gargantua, Interstellar shows that when you get the small details right, the overall experience becomes transcendent.

4. Inspiration Through Learning: As Pratixit shared, he learned complex physics concepts in just one week to prepare for this presentation. The point isn't to master everything—it's the willingness to dive deep into challenging ideas because you're passionate about understanding.

"I've learned so much in preparing this, and I'm sure I'll forget parts of it soon, but the experience is what matters."

— Pratixit, Freshman

Interstellar reminds us that mathematics isn't just abstract numbers—it's a language for describing the universe, from the bending of light around black holes to the flow of time itself. By exploring the math behind what we love, we gain a deeper appreciation for both the art and the reality.
